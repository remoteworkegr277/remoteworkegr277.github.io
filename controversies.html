Controversies

Since their debut into everyday use, videoconferencing technologies have received their fair share of backlash for security and privacy concerns. Just last year in April 2020, BBC reported that Zoom had been accused of transmitting user-data to Facebook and misleading users about guaranteed end-to-end encryption [3].

An ex-NSA hacker warned about the potential for microphone and webcam hijacking on MAC devices [3].

High-confidentiality government institutions, include NASA, prohibited employees from using Zoom [1].

That same month, the Verge and Bloomberg published reports on Zoom disclosing personal information to third parties. But even today, Zoom is continuing to work out the kinks. On April 2, Zoom released a new feature that “hides user personal information in contact details. The affected fields are: personal note, company, department, manager, job title, locations, internal number, extension, direct number, phone number, email.” That’s a lot, but privacy is not the only concern.

Microsoft Teams has also had their fair share of backlash for controversial features. TechRadar expressed concerns on Microsoft allowing meeting moderators to censor communication in a meeting such as deleting certain chat messages which could potentially lead to serious miscommunication and loss of context.
